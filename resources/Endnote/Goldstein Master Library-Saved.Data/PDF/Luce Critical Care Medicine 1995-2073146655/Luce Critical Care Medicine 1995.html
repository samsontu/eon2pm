Abstract
 

Objective: This article was written to argue that physicians are not ethically obligated to provide care which they consider futile, unreasonable, or both, either voluntarily or in response to patient or surrogate demands.

 

Data Sources: Data used to prepare this article were drawn from published articles, including original investigations, position papers, and editorials in the author's personal files.

 

Study Selection: Articles were selected for their relevance to the subjects of medical ethics, the concepts of futility and medical reasonableness, case law, and healthcare reform.

 

Data Extraction: The author extracted all applicable data.

 

Data Synthesis: Physicians may feel obligated to provide care in all clinical circumstances due to the single master view of medicine and the ethical principle of autonomy. However, care may be considered futile according to several definitions of that word, including that which describes futile treatment as something that does not benefit the patient as a whole. Furthermore, care may be considered unreasonable if it is excessive and not generally agreed upon. Physician refusal to provide futile or unreasonable care is supported by the ethical principles of nonmaleficence, beneficence, and distributive justice. The last principle is particularly relevant in the current climate of healthcare reform.

 

Conclusions: Although the issue of physician refusal of requested care has not been resolved by case law or legal statute, it is supported by compelling ethical principles. Physicians are not ethically required to provide futile or unreasonable care, especially to patients who are brain dead, vegetative, critically or terminally ill with little chance of recovery, and unlikely to benefit from cardiopulmonary resuscitation.

 

(Crit Care Med 1995; 23:760-766)

 

 

KEY WORDS: medical ethics; physician-patient relationship; life-support systems; right to die; physician's role; terminal care; critical illness; brain death

ETHICAL PRINCIPLES
 

Medical ethics is a set of moral principles that govern the behavior of physicians and other health professionals. As discussed by Beauchamp and Childress [1], one principle of medical ethics is autonomy: respect for the patient's capacity of self-determination and exercise of personal, informed choice. Another is nonmaleficence: the obligation not to inflict harm intentionally. A third principle is beneficence: the provision of benefit, which generally is defined as something that promotes well-being. A fourth is distributive justice: the fair, equitable, and appropriate distribution of medical resources in society.

 

These four ethical principles have evolved over time and are not immutable. Nevertheless, they provide a moral framework for making medical decisions, including those decisions that involve the administration of life-sustaining therapies [2,3]. Historically, such decisions were made by physicians operating under the principles of nonmaleficence and beneficence. These principles allowed physicians to do what they considered best for patients, including not providing care for persons who were "overmastered by their diseases,'' in the words of Hippocrates [4]. This stance was in keeping with the fact that physicians could offer few beneficial therapies to the critically ill.

The Single Master View of Medicine.
 

Increasingly in the United States, medical decisions have been made by patients and their surrogates under the principle of autonomy. One reason for this shift is that more potentially beneficial therapies are now available, and patients and surrogates, especially those individuals with third-party coverage, expect access to them. Another reason is that physicians have encouraged patients' and surrogates' expectations. Fee-forservice reimbursement has reinforced this encouragement on the part of physicians, who generally have profited from the care they deliver. Nevertheless, it has been prompted primarily by the physicians' deep sense of obligation to the patients for whom they care.

 

This sense of obligation is captured in what might be called the "single master'' view of medicine. As articulated by Levinsky [5], this view holds that physicians should not be providers and rationers of medical services simultaneously. Instead, they "... are required to do everything that they believe may benefit each patient without regard to costs or other social considerations. In caring for an individual patient, the doctor must act solely as that patient's advocate, against the apparent interests of society as a whole, if necessary.'' The single master view has been very compelling to physicians in the United States. It has influenced them to provide many kinds of care, including that which may be considered futile, either voluntarily or in response to patient and surrogate demands.

Concept of Futility.
 

The English word "futile'' was derived from the Latin root futilis, meaning that which easily pours or melts. This root, in turn, stemmed from a Greek myth in which the daughters of the King of Argos, who murdered their husbands on the instructions of their father, were condemned by the gods to collect water for eternity using leaky sieves. From this story has come the concept that futile acts, including certain medical interventions, are ineffective and incapable of achieving a desired result or goal.

 

Younger [6] offered several kinds of goals that may or may not be achieved by medical interventions. One kind of goal is physiologic, for example, the raising of blood pressure by vasoactive agents. Another is post-poning death by the administration of antibiotics or other means. A third goal is lengthening life for some period, such as by the use of mechanical ventilation. A fourth goal is improving the quality of life, so that the patient whose life is prolonged can also live independently. Presumably, an intervention could be deemed futile if it failed to meet one or all of these goals.

 
Back to Top 
Quantitating Futility.
 

Schneiderman et al. [7] have taken a more quantitative approach to medical futility. They have proposed that a treatment should be considered futile if it has been useless in the last 100 cases of a physician's personal experience or in published reports. A treatment that merely preserves permanent unconsciousness or cannot end dependence on critical care should also be considered futile. Furthermore, "in judging futility, physicians must distinguish between an effect, which is limited to a part of the patient's body, and a benefit, which appreciably improves the person as a whole.''

 

In recent years, a number of prognostic scoring systems have been developed to assist physicians in determining which patients are most likely to benefit from life-sustaining treatment. These systems, which include the Acute Physiology and Chronic Health Evaluation (APACHE) system, use large databases to predict the hospital mortality of patients who receive critical care. Depending on the decision criteria used, the systems have a positive predictive value of approximate 80%, and a negative predictive value of approximate 90%. These values correlate well with clinical judgment in most series.

 
Back to Top 
Limitations of Futility.
 

The use of prognostic scoring systems such as APACHE in deciding who is likely to benefit from life-sustaining treatment is ethically appropriate because the systems are as reliable as clinical judgment [8]. Nevertheless, using the systems specifically for limiting care was not intended by their developers, and the systems only analyze hospital mortality, not the duration or quality of life after discharge. Furthermore, physicians who use the systems must realize that there is always some chance that an individual patient will survive despite a high likelihood of hospital mortality. The 95% confidence intervals for the mortality of 100 patients are 97 to 100.

 

In addition to the fact that outcome cannot be perfectly predicted, the concept of futility is limited in that physicians, patients, surrogates, and other parties may view futility differently. For example, some patients or their surrogates would settle for extended biological life as a result of treatment, whereas Schneiderman et al. [7] would consider treatment futile if it could not end permanent unconsciousness or dependence on critical care. Furthermore, as noted by Truog et al. [9], arguments based on the concept of futility often contain implicit concerns of resource allocation that might be better addressed if the concerns were made explicitly.

 
Back to Top 
Concept of Reasonableness.
 

Given these and other limitations, the concept of futility should probably be restricted to the original connotation of futilis in describing acts that are ineffective and incapable of producing a desired result. Those people who prefer a very limited use of the concept may limit it to the idea of physiologic futility proposed by Younger [6], although this use does not allow for describing benefit as "that which appreciably improves the patient as a whole,'' as advocated by Schneiderman et al [7]. If the word "futile'' is used to convey a meaning such as the limited likelihood of prolonging life or improving its quality, this meaning should be spelled out, and some quantitative notion of the likelihood (not necessarily zero) should be used. Alternatively, futility should be replaced or augmented by another word that provides a concept that is applicable in more circumstances.

 

One word that might replace or augment "futile'' is "reasonable,'' meaning rational, not excessive, and generally agreed upon. The concept of reasonableness can be applied both to medical interventions and to the benefits sought by such interventions. Reasonableness allows for more latitude than futility in that interventions considered unreasonable need not always be ineffective, and benefits considered unreasonable need not be incapable of being achieved. In this regard, Jonsen [10] noted that "... The old tutorist rule requires a physician to supply a procedure that offers any chance of saving life. A more relaxed rule would allow the selection of a level of probability or a reasonable chance. It would remove from the moral vocabulary of medicine the phrase `Do everything' and substitute `Do as much as is reasonable.'''

 
Back to Top 
Defining What Is Reasonable.
 

Although the concept of reasonableness allows for more latitude than futility does, people still may define what is reasonable differently. Ultimately then, the key issue with both futility and reasonableness is not so much the definitions of the words, but rather, whose definitions are accepted. Physicians are allowed to define physiologic futility entirely because they possess the requisite scientific knowledge. Tomlinson and Brody [11] also have argued that only physicians can decide what constitutes reasonable medical practice. This decision-making is appropriate because physicians have "the moral authority to judge not just the harms, but also the benefits of medical interventions. This authority flows from the social concern with their professional rather than their personal or individual integrity.''

 

The essence of Tomlinson and Brody's [11] argument is that physicians are, and should be, allowed to define what is reasonable because they have been empowered to do so by society in exchange for meeting the population's medical needs. This empowerment is made possible because, as members of a profession, physicians are expected to set high standards, hold all patients' interests in trust, and police themselves. However, society increasingly has questioned the moral authority, the willingness to treat all people, and the self-regulation of the medical profession. At the same time, the ethical principle of autonomy has come into more conflict with the principles of nonmaleficence and beneficence. Nowhere is this conflict more apparent than in recent case law.

ILLUSTRATIVE CASES
 
Back to Top 
Karen Ann Quinlan.
 

In re Quinlan [12] was the first case to define situations in which life-sustaining therapy could be removed under the principle of autonomy. This case involved a 22-yr-old woman who was being maintained in a persistent vegetative state on a mechanical ventilator in a New Jersey hospital. When the hospital and Ms. Quinlan's physicians refused to remove the ventilator at her parents' request, her father petitioned the court to be named her guardian with the power to have the ventilator removed. The lower court denied the petition, but the New Jersey Supreme Court reversed its decision.

 

In its opinion, the New Jersey Supreme Court stated that patients generally would accept or reject treatment on the basis of its ability to support sentient life rather than mere biological existence. Having considered that Ms. Quinlan, if she had been capable of making medical decisions herself, would have forgone therapy, the court decided that her right to privacy would be abrogated if it prevented the exercise of that right on her behalf. The court therefore granted the father's petition, allowing him to exercise substituted judgment for his daughter. However, it did not address the issue of whether physicians could override patient or surrogate insistence that nonsentient life be maintained, because this was not Mr. Quinlan's desire.

 
Back to Top 
Claire Conroy.
 

In re Conroy [13] provided guidelines for physician refusal of care. This case involved an 83-yr-old nursing home resident with severe dementia and numerous chronic medical problems who was being fed by a nasogastric tube. When her nephew and guardian sought permission to remove the tube on the grounds that this approach would have been his aunt's wish, the trial court agreed to his petition. It did so on the grounds that Mrs. Conroy's life had become permanently burdensome, despite the fact that she might die a painful death due to dehydration and starvation.

 

A first appellate court revised this trial court's decision after reasoning that removing the nasogastric tube would constitute an impermissible killing. However, on appeal, the New Jersey Supreme Court held that any medical treatment, including hydration or nutrition, may be withheld or withdrawn from an incompetent patient for either of two reasons: a) it is clear that the patient would have refused the treatment if he or she were autonomous, that is, capable of exercising informed choice; or b) the treatment is not in the patient's best interest in that the burdens of the treatment outweighed the benefits that the patient derived from life. Although the court did not declare how patient interests might be determined, it did provide a standard by which refusal of requested care might be justified.

 
Back to Top 
Baby L.
 

Another case that touched on the issue of physician refusal of care was that of Baby L [14]. This case involved a 2-yr-old girl with severe neurologic handicaps who suffered uncontrolled seizures and recurrent episodes of aspiration pneumonia. After repeated hospitalizations, the infant was readmitted to Boston Children's Hospital and required mechanical ventilation and cardiovascular support. The physicians caring for Baby L believed that she could experience only pain and that further medical treatment would not be of benefit. However, the infant's mother, who had long demanded that everything be done to ensure her daughter's survival, rejected this opinion and requested more care.

 

Baby L stabilized without mechanical ventilation while the physicians sought a facility to care for her. In the meantime, the mother's attorney arranged for a hearing in the probate court. The judge asked the physicians what they would do if the court issued an order for mechanical ventilation and was told that this treatment would not be provided because it would violate the physicians' professional obligation to the patient. The court then appointed a guardian ad litem for Baby L, who subsequently was transferred to another institution. In the process, the issue of whether physicians could refuse surrogate wishes was not resolved.

 
Back to Top 
Helen Wanglie.
 

The case of Helen Wanglie [15] involved an 85-yr-old woman who had a cardiorespiratory arrest during an attempt at weaning from mechanical ventilation at a chronic care facility. She was returned to Hennepin County Medical Center in a persistent vegetative state. In the summer of 1990, Mrs. Wanglie's physicians met with her husband and children and recommended that life-sustaining therapy, which was costing approximate $500,000 a year, be withdrawn. The husband said that the patient had never expressed her wishes regarding life support, but that he and the children insisted that therapy be continued because they valued life over death and because a miracle might occur.

 

In the fall of 1990, Mrs. Wanglie's new attending physician, with the consent of the Medical Center Ethics Committee, told the family that he was not willing to continue mechanical ventilation because it was not beneficial, since it could not heal Mrs. Wanglie's lungs or end her unconsciousness; because ventilation could prolong her life, it was not described as physiologically futile. In 1991, after the family had tried and failed to find another facility willing to care for the patient, the Medical Center asked the court first to appoint an independent conservator to decide if ventilation was beneficial, and second, for a hearing to determine whether ventilation was required if the conservator held that it was not beneficial. Mr. Wanglie crossfiled, requesting that he be appointed conservator. The court agreed with his request on the grounds that he could best represent his wife's interests. Three days later, while receiving full support at the Medical Center, Mrs. Wanglie died. The court never addressed the question of whether physicians or the Medical Center could refuse to provide requested treatment, and thus, the conflict between nonmaleficence and beneficence and autonomy was not resolved.

 
Back to Top 
Baby K.
 

The most recent case illustrating the conflict between autonomy and nonmaleficence and beneficence was that of Baby K [16]. This case involved an anencephalic infant whose mother insisted that she receive mechanical ventilation whenever she had trouble breathing. Baby K's physicians disagreed, but they could not find another hospital willing to take their patient. Although Baby K ultimately was weaned from the ventilator and discharged to a nursing home, she required readmission on several occasions. Following her second readmission, the hospital sought court agreement that it was not required to provide mechanical ventilation. A federal judge ruled against the hospital, which in turn appealed the decision to the Fourth Circuit Court in Virginia. This appeal was based, in large part, on a Virginia statute stating that physicians are not required "to prescribe or render medical treatment'' they deem "medically or ethically inappropriate'' [17].

 

The Circuit Court denied this appeal and affirmed the lower court's decision on the basis that the hospital and physicians were required to provide requested care under the federal Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act of 1986, which preempted the Virginia state statute. The federal Act, which was designed primarily to prevent patient "dumping,'' requires hospitals either to stabilize patients who seek emergency treatment or transfer them if absolutely necessary. The Appeals Court reasoned that Baby K had an emergency condition, namely, "bradypnea or apnea, not anencephaly,'' that the hospital was required to treat with mechanical ventilation. Thus, the issue of Baby K's treatment was decided on a strict reading of the Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act. Whether anencephalic infants should receive mechanical ventilation indefinitely, or whether other patients with similarly hopeless conditions should be supported, was not dealt with [17].

ETHICAL RESOLUTION
 

It can be argued that the court decisions in cases such as Helen Wanglie and Baby K have tended to reinforce the principle of autonomy over the principles of nonmaleficence and beneficence. Yet, the decisions all fall short of resolving the ethical conflict between these principles. Legal resolution of this issue seems unlikely at this writing because a relevant court case is not pending and because legislatures are ill equipped to determine what constitutes appropriate medical treatment. As a result, physicians must rely on ethical principles in justifying the refusal of requested care.

 

Those physicians who believe that Mrs. Wanglie's and Baby K's physicians should have refused their surrogates' request can call not only upon the principles of nonmaleficence and beneficence but also upon the principle of distributive justice. This position is tenable because Mrs. Wanglie and Baby K were consuming critical care services, paid for in part by public dollars, that could have been used to support other patients, critically ill, or otherwise. In this regard, Miles [18], an ethicist who advised the Hennepin County Medical Center in the Helen Wanglie case, noted that "In this case, the husband's request seemed entirely inconsistent with what medical care could do for his wife, the standards of the community, and his fair share of medical resources that many people pooled for their collective medical care.''

HEALTHCARE REFORM
 

The position that providing futile or unreasonable treatment may violate the principle of distributive justice is particularly relevant in the context of current proposals for healthcare reform. Although these proposals differ, most of them are designed to provide access to basic health benefits, including preventive and primary care services, for all American citizens. The proposals also call for reducing the costs of health care through mechanisms such as global budgets and managed competition. Central to managed competition are managed care plans containing primary care physicians who restrict the use of expensive tests, therapies, and consultants, and who are reimbursed on a capitated rather than a fee-for-service basis.

 

Whether and how these current reform proposals will survive Congressional debate remains unclear. Nevertheless, the steady growth of capitation and managed care indicates that healthcare reform is becoming a reality independent of government action. Rationing of medical services probably will be part of this reality, and such rationing is less unlikely to be based on the economic leverage and vocal insistence of a minority of patients and surrogates than it has been in the past. As Miles [18] pointed out in reference to the Helen Wanglie case, "We could not afford a universal health care system based on patients' demands. Such systems would irrationally allocate health care to socially powerful people with strong preferences for immediate treatment to the disadvantage of those with less power or less immediate needs.''

 
Back to Top 
Proportional Advocacy.
 

Physicians who subscribe to the single master view of medicine are understandably concerned that their traditional duty to patients may be compromised under healthcare reform. On the one hand, managed competition may not allow physicians "to do everything that they believe may benefit each patient without regard to costs or social considerations,'' in Levinsky's [5] words. On the other hand, physicians may be required to demonstrate that the things they do produce a benefit "which appreciably improves the patient as a whole,'' as called for by Schneiderman et al [7]. This requirement may be particularly difficult for critical care physicians, many of whose efforts are expensive and of unproven long-term value.

 

Although concerns about compromises of physician duty are understandable, the single master view need not be so compelling as it has been in influencing physician behavior. This change is because medical practice is governed by all four ethical principles, and historical events dictate that the influence of autonomy be diminished to accommodate distributive justice. It is therefore proper that physicians simultaneously seek to broaden access to health care and balance their advocacy of individual patients with what Jonsen [10] has called "proportional advocacy, whereby every patient is not merely this presenting patient but all potential patients, and whereby the advocacy argues not for `everything possible' but for everything `probably beneficial.'''

 
Back to Top 
Building a Consensus.
 

Proportional advocacy on the part of physicians must be complemented by proportional advocacy on the part of patients if the egalitarian and utilitarian goals of healthcare reform are to be realized. In the process, patients and surrogates must restrict their self-advocacy to what is fair for all people, just as physicians must help define what medical treatment is reasonable. Such definition can best be reached by the kind of professional consensus represented in groups like the Task Force on Ethics of the Society of Critical Care Medicine [19] and the Bioethics Task Force of the American Thoracic Society [20].

 

In addition to developing professional consensus, physicians also must help society reach consensus on what constitutes reasonable care. They must do so because the moral authority of physicians has been diminished in the United States, and because issues such as medical reasonableness should involve all of society. Achieving social consensus will not be easy, given the lack of legal guidelines and the expectations of many patients and surrogates that they can have unlimited therapies. Nevertheless, the chances of achieving consensus are perhaps greater than at any other time in our history, and they will be improved if physicians publicly state the circumstances under which they feel that they are not obligated to provide requested care.

LIMITS OF THE PHYSICIAN'S OBLIGATION
 

Brett and McCullough [21], among others, described three circumstances in which requested care is not required. The first circumstance is when such care is not likely to confer benefit, as might be the case if a patient demanded antibiotics for a viral upper respiratory tract infection. The second is when the care is likely to cause more harm than good, as might be the case if a patient with insomnia sought large doses of barbiturates. The third circumstance is when the request, although not necessarily harmful and without benefit, conflicts with distributive justice, as might be the case if a patient with chronic tension headaches wanted a cerebral computed tomography scan. Although the last patient might be permitted a scan if he or she could pay for it, the scan should not be allowed if it would deprive another medically needy patient of the procedure.

 

These examples all involve comparatively healthy outpatients who are not already receiving the diagnostic or therapeutic modalities they request. Furthermore, although the modalities are important to the patients for real or symbolic reasons, they are not life-sustaining. However, the refusal of a given intervention is much more difficult to physicians when life is at stake, particularly if the intervention already is underway. This sentiment is echoed by Lantos et al. [22]: "When the chance of success is low, but the alternative to treatment is death, and the patient desires therapy, the presumption should be in favor of treatment.''

Brain Death.
 

Lantos et al. [22] noted that unsuccessful therapeutic trials often lead critically ill patients and their surrogates to relinquish unreasonable goals or abandon futile treatments. Most patients and surrogates eventually accept physician recommendations to limit life-sustaining therapy if they have not agreed to do so initially. However, such agreement does not occur in all circumstances, no matter how many discussions are held. When it does not, physicians may consider unilaterally refusing or not offering care either because it is futile or unreasonable.

 

One situation in which unilateral refusal of care is appropriate would involve a brain-dead patient whose family requests further mechanical ventilation because they believe the patient is alive. Despite the presence of heartbeat and chest wall excursions, such a patient is legally dead and lacks the coordinated central nervous system function necessary to sustain biological life. Mechanical ventilation therefore is both futile (ineffective in restoring life), and unreasonable (irrational and excessive) for the patient, and the ventilator should be withdrawn regardless of the family's wishes. According to the consensus statement of the Task Force on Ethics of the Society of Critical Care Medicine [19], "It is inappropriate in this situation to seek the agreement of the family or the surrogate to the withdrawal of medical support,'' although they should be told why support is being withdrawn.

Persistent Vegetative State.
 

The persistent vegetative state is characterized by unawareness of the self and the environment, with preservation of brainstem function. Recovery of consciousness from such a state is unlikely, although not impossible, and the life span of vegetative patients is markedly reduced [23]. The Society of Critical Care Medicine Consensus Task Force [19] considered continued critical care for vegetative patients futile and recommended that vegetative patients should not be maintained in the intensive care unit (ICU) to the exclusion of other patients who could derive more benefit. A similar position was taken by the Bioethics Task Force of the American Thoracic Society [20], which considered care futile if it would not restore sentient function.

 

Life-sustaining therapy is not futile in the physiologic sense because it may stabilize a patient's vital signs. Nevertheless, such therapy does not benefit patients as a whole because sentient function is not restored. Furthermore, therapy is unreasonable because most Americans do not desire such care, according to public opinion polls. Angell [24] stated that physicians should assume that vegetative patients would not want to be kept alive indefinitely unless evidence exists to the contrary. "On this basis,'' she has written, "we could establish a standard of care that included routinely stopping treatment after a specified time in a persistent vegetative state.''

Other Critically Ill Patients.
 

The idea that physicians should provide treatment when death is the alternative does not seem applicable to vegetative patients. However, the futility or unreasonableness of care is less obvious in other critically ill patients who do not have severe central nervous system damage. Patients with problems such as irreversible respiratory or hepatic failure may request therapy. More commonly, because the patients are unconscious, therapy is requested by surrogates who may or may not ask for things the patients would if they could express their own wishes. Under what conditions can therapy be denied?

 

Although the stakes are admittedly higher for these persons than for healthy outpatients, care can be withheld from critically ill patients in the same circumstances outlined by Brett and McCullough [21]. For example, therapy is not required if it is not beneficial, as might be the case with empiric antibiotics in patients not known to be infected. Therapy also is not required if it causes more harm than benefit, as with painful, invasive, or risky surgical procedures that are unlikely to improve outcome. Finally, care is not required if it could be better used by other critically ill patients. In this regard, Truog [25] argued that extracorporeal membrane oxygenation may reasonably be removed from a child with a poor prognosis and given to another child whose prognosis is better if resources are limited.

 
Back to Top 
Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR).
 

The issue of whether to attempt CPR arises frequently in patients in and outside of the ICU. Although the outcomes from CPR may improve with time, studies to date have shown that this therapy is not successful in most patients, particularly the elderly [26] and those patients hospitalized with diseases such as pneumonia [27]. In addition, many patients in whom a viable cardiac rhythm is restored spend their last days of life in the ICU with depressed central nervous system function. Despite this outcome, some terminally ill patients or their surrogates request CPR, presumably because it represents a last chance of life. When they cannot be persuaded to change this position, are physicians required to perform CPR?

 

CPR need not be physiologically futile for patients such as those patients with metastic cancer or multiple organ failure, since they may regain a heartbeat. Nevertheless, CPR cannot prolong their lives for more than a short time or improve its quality. Furthermore, attempting CPR is both unreasonable and professionally objectionable because it promises more than medicine can deliver. In this regard, Blackhall [28] argued that CPR should not be offered to terminally ill patients or their surrogates. "In cases like these, in which CPR offers no conceivable benefit and much possible harm, I believe that patient autonomy cannot be our only guide.''
